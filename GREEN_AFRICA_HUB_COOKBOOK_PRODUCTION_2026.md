## Introduction: The Mathematics of Sustainable Cooking

### Why Carbon Footprint Matters in Cooking

Every meal we prepare tells a story—not just of culture and tradition, but of environmental impact. The ingredients we choose, the cooking methods we use, and the energy we consume all contribute to our personal carbon footprint. In the UK, where food accounts for approximately 20% of household emissions, understanding these impacts becomes crucial for climate action.

### The African Advantage

African and Caribbean cuisines have inherently sustainable characteristics that make them ideal for low-carbon cooking:

- **Plant-forward traditions**: Many traditional dishes are naturally vegetarian or use meat as a flavor enhancer rather than the main component
- **Local adaptation**: These cuisines have always adapted to local ingredients and seasonal availability
- **Efficient cooking**: Traditional methods often maximize flavor while minimizing energy use
- **Minimal waste**: Root-to-stem and nose-to-tail principles have long been practiced

### The Science Behind the Numbers

This cookbook uses the latest 2026 data from UK government sources to calculate the carbon impact of each recipe. Our calculations consider:

1. **Food Production**: Emissions from farming, processing, and packaging
2. **Transport**: Distance and method of transportation from source to UK
3. **Cooking Energy**: Electricity or gas consumption during preparation
4. **Storage**: Refrigeration and freezing requirements

### The Induction Revolution

The most significant finding in our research is the dramatic impact of cooking method choice. Induction cooking, combined with the UK's increasingly clean electricity grid (124g CO₂/kWh in 2024), reduces cooking emissions by 67% compared to traditional gas hobs.

This single choice—switching to induction—can reduce the carbon footprint of many recipes by up to 200g CO₂e per serving, making it the most impactful individual change a home cook can make.

## How to Use This Cookbook

### Carbon Badge System

Each recipe features a carbon badge showing its environmental impact:

**Low Impact** (Under 300g CO₂e): Plant-based dishes and efficient cooking
**Medium Impact** (300-800g CO₂e): Balanced meals with moderate meat content
**High Impact** (Over 800g CO₂e): Meat-heavy dishes or special occasion meals

### Cooking Method Indicators

**Induction Optimized**: Best results with lowest carbon impact
**Gas Compatible**: Traditional method with higher carbon footprint
**Plant-Based**: Naturally vegetarian or vegan
**Sustainable Seafood**: MSC-certified or low-impact fish choices

### Serving Size Notes

All carbon calculations are based on:
- Standard adult serving size
- Induction cooking where specified
- UK-sourced ingredients where available
- Sea freight transport for imported items

### Scaling Impact

Carbon footprint scales approximately linearly with serving size, but cooking energy doesn't double when you cook for two instead of one. Consider batch cooking to maximize efficiency.

## Methodology: Carbon Calculation Explained

### Updated with 2026 UK Government Data

This cookbook uses the most current carbon emission factors available, updated in April 2026 with the latest UK government statistics.

### Emission Factors Used

#### Transport Emissions (DEFRA 2024)
- Air freight: 2,100 g CO₂/tonne-km
- Sea freight: 10 g CO₂/tonne-km
- Road freight: 100 g CO₂/tonne-km
- Local delivery: 180 g CO₂/tonne-km

#### UK Electricity Grid
- 2024: 124 g CO₂/kWh (cleanest ever recorded)
- Grid improvement: 11% reduction from 2023

#### Food Production (UK-specific 2024)
- Beef: 27 kg CO₂e/kg
- Lamb: 20 kg CO₂e/kg
- Goat: 5 kg CO₂e/kg
- Chicken: 6.9 kg CO₂e/kg
- Mackerel: 0.7 kg CO₂e/kg
- Rice: 4.0 kg CO₂e/kg
- Potatoes: 0.3 kg CO₂e/kg
- Cassava flour: 0.9 kg CO₂e/kg

#### Cooking Efficiency
- Induction: 87.5% efficiency
- Gas Hob: 47.5% efficiency
- Gas carbon factor: 202 g CO₂/kWh

### Calculation Method

For each recipe, we calculate:

1. **Ingredient Footprint**: (Weight × Production Factor) + Transport Emissions
2. **Cooking Energy**: (Energy Used × Grid Carbon Factor) ÷ Efficiency
3. **Total Impact**: Sum of all components

### Key Findings

- **Induction advantage**: 67% reduction in cooking emissions confirmed
- **Sea freight dominance**: 210x cleaner than air freight
- **UK sourcing benefits**: Local ingredients significantly lower impact
- **Recipe rankings**: Relative impact between recipes remains consistent

### 1. Jollof Rice
**Carbon Badge**: Medium Impact  
**Carbon Footprint**: 371g CO₂e per serving  
**Cooking Time**: 50 minutes  
**Servings**: 6  
**Cooked on**: Energy-Efficient Induction Hob

#### The Story:
Jollof Rice is the crown jewel of West African cooking — a deeply seasoned, tomato-based one-pot rice dish that unites Nigeria, Ghana, Senegal and the diaspora. Every family has their version. This is the Green Africa Hub edition: made with love, cooked on induction, and already one of the lowest-carbon dishes in the book.

#### Allergens:

| Salt to taste | UK — zero miles | 0.0g |

ALL INGREDIENTS SEA-SHIPPED OR UK-SOURCED — No air-freight swaps needed. Fully authentic and already low transport carbon.

#### Method — Induction Hob (any brand):
Use the induction settings guide on page 2 if your hob uses LOW / MEDIUM / HIGH labels.

1. Heat palm oil — INDUCTION: Setting 8/9 (HIGH) / ~180°C / 5 mins until shimmering.
2. Fry sliced onion until golden, ~5 mins. Add tomato purée, fry 2 mins.
3. Add blended tomato paste — INDUCTION: Setting 6 (MEDIUM-HIGH) / ~130°C / 20 mins. Stir occasionally until raw tomato smell fades and oil rises.
4. Add stock cubes, spices, bay leaves and stock. Stir well. Season generously.
5. Add washed rice, stir to coat. INDUCTION: Setting 4 (MEDIUM-LOW) / ~100°C / 25–30 mins. Cover tightly.
6. DO NOT lift the lid for 25 mins. Check rice — cooked through with a slight smoky base is the 'party Jollof' effect.
7. Smart cooker / food processor (if using): blend tomatoes and peppers on high speed before cooking.

#### Smart Cooker / Food Processor Tip:
Use any blender or food processor to blend tomatoes and peppers before cooking. A hand blender works perfectly.

### 2. Pounded Yam & Spinach Lamb Stew
**Carbon Badge**: High Impact  
**Carbon Footprint**: 2,776g CO₂e per serving  
**Cooking Time**: 90 minutes  
**Servings**: 4  
**Cooked on**: Energy-Efficient Induction Hob

#### The Story:
Pounded yam is a cornerstone of Yoruba and Igbo cuisine — smooth, stretchy, and deeply satisfying. Here it meets a rich Bristol-inspired lamb stew where UK-raised lamb replaces imported bushmeat. Spinach stands in for air-freighted ugwu, cutting transport carbon by 99.9% while delivering the same iron-rich depth.

#### Allergens:
Celery (stock cubes). Contains: none of the 14 major UK allergens. Check stock cube labels.

#### Ingredients & Transport Carbon:
| Ingredient | Origin & Shipping Mode | Transport CO₂ |
|------------|----------------------|----------------|
| 800g white yam, peeled & cubed | Ghana/Nigeria → UK (sea) | 64.0g |
| 500g lamb shoulder, diced | UK-raised (road) | 5.0g |
| 200g fresh spinach (UK-grown) | UK (road) Green Swap | 1.6g |
| 400g tinned tomatoes | Italy → UK (road) | 60.0g |
| 2 tbsp red palm oil (~28g) | Nigeria → UK (sea) | 1.4g |
| 1 large onion (200g, UK) | UK (road) | 1.6g |
| 2 scotch bonnet peppers (50g) | UK greenhouse (road) | 0.8g |
| 2 stock cubes (20g) | UK (road) | 0.6g |
| 1 tsp curry powder, thyme (8g) | India → UK (sea) | 0.6g |
| Salt & black pepper to taste | UK — zero miles | 0.0g |

GREEN AFRICA HUB AIR-FREIGHT SWAP:
Fresh ugwu/pumpkin leaves (Nigeria (air, 5,000km)): 2100g CO₂
UK-grown fresh spinach (UK (road, 80km)): 1.6g CO₂
Saving: 2098g CO₂ — 99.9% reduction for this ingredient alone

#### Method — Induction Hob (any brand):
Use the induction settings guide on page 2 if your hob uses LOW / MEDIUM / HIGH labels.

1. Season lamb with salt, pepper, curry powder and scotch bonnet paste. Leave 10 mins.
2. INDUCTION: Setting 8 (HIGH) / ~180°C / 8 mins — brown lamb in palm oil in batches until sealed on all sides.
3. Add onion, fry 3 mins. Add tomato purée and blended scotch bonnet.
4. Add tinned tomatoes, stock cube, curry powder, thyme and 200ml water.
5. INDUCTION: Setting 4 (MEDIUM-LOW) / ~95°C / 70 mins — cover and simmer until lamb is very tender. Stir occasionally.
6. Add spinach in the last 5 mins. Stir until wilted. Taste and season.
7. For pounded yam: boil yam in salted water — INDUCTION: Setting 7 (MEDIUM-HIGH) / 20 mins until fork-tender.
8. Drain completely. Pound with pestle and mortar, or blend in a food processor, adding warm water 1 tbsp at a time until smooth and stretchy.
9. Serve stew alongside pounded yam with a generous ladle of rich sauce.

#### Smart Cooker / Food Processor Tip:
After boiling yam, a hand mixer or food processor can replace the pestle and mortar for pounding. Add warm water gradually.
